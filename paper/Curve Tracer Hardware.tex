\begin{abstract}
When developing systems for perovskite solar cells, it's important to have a testing platform that closely resembles your deployed systems.  While developing a 1U CubeSat, the Big Red Sat-1, at the University of Nebraska-Lincoln, we found a lack of passive curve tracing instrumentation, which we needed for our mission.  To that end, we developed a benchtop passive curve tracer that leverages the Ossila Solar Simulator as an AM1.0 light source.  This device utilizes a ladder of switched resistors and a precision voltage and current measurement to achieve the curve tracing.  It also integrates a common-anode multiplexer to switch through multi-pixel perovskite solar cells.  Finally, the solar cell holder is separate from the main measurement system, so various substrates sizes and pixel counts can be supported.  This system is tested using various perovskite solar cells.  It has been open sourced to enable reuse in future systems that may benefit from the lower power consumption and flight heritage.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}

Perovskite solar cells (PSCs) have demonstrated the fastest efficiency scaling of any solar power device \cite{velilla2019outdoor}.  Both terrestrial and space applications benefit from the low manufacturing cost, recyclability, and high power density of PSCs \cite{kirmani2023metal, mcmillon2022would}.  Additionally, the absorber material can be transported to off-world surfaces, where 12 kilograms of material can fabricate a 12 MW solar array \cite{mcmillon2022would}.  To assess the performance of PSCs in real-world environments, testing apparatuses need to be developed and deployed to measure key parameters.  Many key parameters of PSCs can be captured by curve tracing.

A curve tracer measures the development of voltage and current coming from, or going into, a device while varying the load conditions, the resulting curve being termed an I-V curve \cite{zhu2020comprehensive}.  These two variables are plotted with voltage on the x-axis and current on the y-axis, yielding a variety of common shapes.  The modulation of load conditions is a well known problem in electrical engineering and there are many solutions for curve tracers, including a capacitive load, an electronic load, and a resistive load \cite{zhu2020comprehensive}.  All topologies utilize a voltage measurement block across the load and a current measurement block in line with the solar panel.  The capacitive load has a capacitor attached to the solar panel and the charge cycle of the capacitor allows the curve to be captured \cite{aguilar2017charging}.  The electronic load method uses a MOSFET in the triode region to create a variable resistance, so a modulated gate signal proportionally modulates the load \cite{sahbel2013experimental}.  Finally, the variable resistance method is very similar to the electronic load method, but discrete resistors are used along with switches to connect and disconnect those resistors from the load \cite{rivai2014binary}. 

Curve tracing during real-world operation of solar cells can indicate their health and monitor for failure conditions \cite{zhu2020comprehensive}.  Any degradation or change in performance of the solar cells will produce a signature in the I-V curve \cite{zhu2020comprehensive, arce2024big}, which when paired with other environmental conditions can indicate how failures happen \cite{silverman2025durability, fisher2025measurement}.  When transitioning PSCs from the laboratory to the field, various experiments will be performed in a variety of environments.  Some environments, like space, introduce constraints on real estate, power budget, and complexity.  To facilitate preparation for practical testing in space environments, we present a low-cost, open-source, benchtop curve tracer for PSCs that was designed for testing PSCs during the Big Red Sat-1 project \cite{arce2024big}.

\section*{Methods}

\subsection*{Curve Tracer}

The basic building blocks of a curve tracer consists of a voltage measurement circuit, current measurement circuit, and a load modulating circuit \cite{zhu2020comprehensive}.  The first two circuits are straightforward to implement.  Considering the PSC pixels have an open circuit voltage around 1V, the voltage measurement can be accomplished using a voltage-follower operational amplifier circuit.  Similarly, current measurements can be performed using a series shunt resistor and measuring the burden voltage to calculate the loop current.  

To vary the load, the variable resistance method is implemented.  This method provides the means to control the precision of the I-V points and can simplify the system design and control.  The main design consideration is the selection of a switch to select, or bypass, the resistor.  If eight resistors and switches are chained together, then 8-bits of control are available, meaning that 256 I-V points are possible.  Figure \ref{fig:curvetracer} shows the two main load configurations.

The open circuit condition for a PSC generates maximum voltage and minimum current, while the short circuit condition generates minimum voltage and maximum current.

\subsection*{Common-Anode Multiplexer}

Generally, a curve tracer for a single solar cell is not as practical as one that can curve trace multiple in a single system and isolate the individual cells.  This can be accomplished in field systems by using a DPDT relay that has the normally closed node connected to the solar charge controller and the normally open node connected to the curve tracer \cite{lopez2021design, pulungan2022design}.  This works well for terrestrial systems, where an atmosphere of pressure can be roughly assumed.  This also works well for solar cells that have a single positive and negative lead that is strung together in a system with a common cathode, so removing the positive connection is straightforward.  For laboratory scale perovskite solar cells (PSCs), they are regularly fabricated with multiple power generating "pixels" with a common-anode \cite{arce2024big} due to its p-i-n construction \cite{krishnan2019factors}.  It's common for these PSCs to have six pixels, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:psc}, and a practical curve tracer needs to multiplex the ground connections from each pixel. 

\begin{figure}[!htb]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=7.5cm, height=7.5cm, keepaspectratio]{psc.png}
	\caption{A common configuration for laboratory PSCs, showing six pixels, the metallic contacts, a common-anode contact, and a coverslip for protection from environmental conditions.}
	\label{fig:psc}
\end{figure}

For p-i-n PSCs, the ground node for each pixel has its connection controlled.  Figure \ref{fig:multiplexer} shows the two connection choices.  Since most PSCs have six pixels, the multiplexer would need six switches to control the measurement of the entire PSC sample.

	\centering

	\includegraphics[width=7.5cm, height=7.5cm, keepaspectratio]{measurementcondition.png}
	\caption{Left shows the discharge condition of a PSC using a DPDT relay and right shows the measurement condition of the same.}
	\label{fig:multiplexer}
\end{figure}

PSCs have the potential to degrade from different load conditions and exposure to light causes the generation of iodide vacancies that increases ionic mobility, leading to degradation mechanisms and the alteration of device behavior \cite{guo2021degradation}.  Because of this effect, there should be an electrical connection for removal of charged iodide vacancies.  There are three options: open circuit, short circuit, or maximum power point.  The impact on lifespan of these three conditions was characterized in \cite{domanski2018systematic}, showing that maximum power point is the ideal bias point.  Each condition will be tested.

\subsection*{Light Source}

To test the power generating capabilities of a PSC, a calibrated light source needs to be used.  There are many options on the market, but the smallest and most easily integrated system is the Ossila LED Solar Simulator.  This solar simulator produces a calibrated AM1.5 light that meets the IEC 60904-9:2020 standard using LEDs.  The light meets class AAA in a 15 millimeter diameter area and class ABA in a 25 millimeter diameter area.  Additionally, this solar simulator provides a serial control interface and shutter capabilities.

\subsection*{Software}

The Python programming language will be used to command the curve tracer and receive I-V points from the measurement circuits.  A simple graphical user interface will be designed that allows user control and real-time visualization of I-V curves.  An MSP430 microcontroller from Texas Instruments will be integrated to control the curve tracer and multiplexer, which will be programmed in C++.  A serial interface command set will be implemented that allows hardware control.

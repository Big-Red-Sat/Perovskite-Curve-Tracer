\begin{abstract}
When developing systems for perovskite solar cells, it's important to have a testing platform that closely resembles your deployed systems.  While developing a 1U CubeSat, the Big Red Sat-1, at the University of Nebraska-Lincoln, we found a lack of passive curve tracing instrumentation, which we needed for our mission.  To that end, we developed a benchtop passive curve tracer that leverages the Ossila Solar Simulator as an AM1.0 light source.  This device utilizes a ladder of switched resistors and a precision voltage and current measurement to achieve the curve tracing.  It also integrates a common-anode multiplexer to switch through multi-pixel perovskite solar cells.  Finally, the solar cell holder is separate from the main measurement system, so various substrates sizes and pixel counts can be supported.  This system is tested using various perovskite solar cells.  It has been open sourced to enable reuse in future systems that may benefit from the lower power consumption and flight heritage.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}

Perovskite solar cells (PSCs) have demonstrated the fastest efficiency scaling of any solar power device \cite{velilla2019outdoor}.  Both terrestrial and space applications benefit from the low manufacturing cost, recyclability, and high power density of PSCs \cite{kirmani2023metal, mcmillon2022would}.  Additionally, the absorber material can be transported to off-world surfaces, where 12 kilograms of material can fabricate a 12 MW solar array \cite{mcmillon2022would}.  To assess the performance of PSCs in real-world environments, testing apparatuses need to be developed and deployed to measure key parameters.  Many key parameters of PSCs can be captured by curve tracing.

A curve tracer measures the development of voltage and current coming from, or going into, a device while varying the load conditions, the resulting curve being termed an I-V curve \cite{zhu2020comprehensive}.  These two variables are plotted with voltage on the x-axis and current on the y-axis, yielding a variety of common shapes.  The modulation of load conditions is a well known problem in electrical engineering and there are many solutions for curve tracers, including a capacitive load, an electronic load, and a resistive load \cite{zhu2020comprehensive}.  All topologies utilize a voltage measurement block across the load and a current measurement block in line with the solar panel.  The capacitive load has a capacitor attached to the solar panel and the charge cycle of the capacitor allows the curve to be captured \cite{aguilar2017charging}.  The electronic load method uses a MOSFET in the triode region to create a variable resistance, so a modulated gate signal proportionally modulates the load \cite{sahbel2013experimental}.  Finally, the variable resistance method is very similar to the electronic load method, but discrete resistors are used along with switches to connect and disconnect those resistors from the load \cite{rivai2014binary}. 

Curve tracing during real-world operation of solar cells can indicate their health and monitor for failure conditions \cite{zhu2020comprehensive}.  Any degradation or change in performance of the solar cells will produce a signature in the I-V curve \cite{zhu2020comprehensive, arce2024big}, which when paired with other environmental conditions can indicate how failures happen \cite{silverman2025durability, fisher2025measurement}.  When transitioning PSCs from the laboratory to the field, various experiments will be performed in a variety of environments.  Some environments, like space, introduce constraints on real estate, power budget, and complexity.  To facilitate preparation for practical testing in space environments, we present a low-cost, open-source, benchtop curve tracer for PSCs that was designed for testing PSCs during the Big Red Sat-1 project \cite{arce2024big}.

\section*{Methods}

\subsection*{Curve Tracer}

The basic building blocks of a curve tracer consists of a voltage measurement circuit, current measurement circuit, and a load modulating circuit \cite{zhu2020comprehensive}.  The first two circuits are straightforward to implement.  Considering the PSC pixels have an open circuit voltage around 1V, the voltage measurement can be accomplished using a voltage-follower operational amplifier circuit.  Similarly, current measurements can be performed using a series shunt resistor and measuring the burden voltage to calculate the loop current.  

To vary the load, the variable resistance method is implemented.  This method provides the means to control the precision of the I-V points and can simplify the system design and control.  The main design consideration is the selection of a switch to select, or bypass, the resistor.  If eight resistors and switches are chained together, then 8-bits of control are available, meaning that 256 I-V points are possible.  Figure \ref{fig:curvetracer} shows the two main load configurations.

The open circuit condition for a PSC generates maximum voltage and minimum current, while the short circuit condition generates minimum voltage and maximum current.

\subsection*{Common-Anode Multiplexer}

Generally, a curve tracer for a single solar cell is not as practical as one that can curve trace multiple in a single system and isolate the individual cells.  This can be accomplished in field systems by using a DPDT relay that has the normally closed node connected to the solar charge controller and the normally open node connected to the curve tracer \cite{lopez2021design, pulungan2022design}.  This works well for terrestrial systems, where an atmosphere of pressure can be roughly assumed.  This also works well for solar cells that have a single positive and negative lead that is strung together in a system with a common cathode, so removing the positive connection is straightforward.  For laboratory scale perovskite solar cells (PSCs), they are regularly fabricated with multiple power generating "pixels" with a common-anode \cite{arce2024big} due to its p-i-n construction \cite{krishnan2019factors}.  It's common for these PSCs to have six pixels, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:psc}, and a practical curve tracer needs to multiplex the ground connections from each pixel. 

\begin{figure}[!htb]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=7.5cm, height=7.5cm, keepaspectratio]{psc.png}
	\caption{A common configuration for laboratory PSCs, showing six pixels, the metallic contacts, a common-anode contact, and a coverslip for protection from environmental conditions.}
	\label{fig:psc}
\end{figure}

For p-i-n PSCs, the ground node for each pixel has its connection controlled.  Figure \ref{fig:multiplexer} shows the two connection choices.  Since most PSCs have six pixels, the multiplexer would need six switches to control the measurement of the entire PSC sample.  

\begin{figure}[!htb]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=7.5cm, height=7.5cm, keepaspectratio]{dischargecondition.png}
	\includegraphics[width=7.5cm, height=7.5cm, keepaspectratio]{measurementcondition.png}
	\caption{Left shows the discharge condition of a PSC using a DPDT relay and right shows the measurement condition of the same.}
	\label{fig:multiplexer}
\end{figure}

PSCs have the potential to degrade from different load conditions and exposure to light causes the generation of iodide vacancies that increases ionic mobility, leading to degradation mechanisms and the alteration of device behavior \cite{guo2021degradation}.  Because of this effect, there should be an electrical connection for removal of charged iodide vacancies.  There are three options: open circuit, short circuit, or maximum power point.  The impact on lifespan of these three conditions was characterized in \cite{domanski2018systematic}, showing that maximum power point is the ideal bias point.  Each condition will be tested.

\subsection*{Light Source}

To test the power generating capabilities of a PSC, a calibrated light source needs to be used.  There are many options on the market, but the smallest and most easily integrated system is the Ossila LED Solar Simulator.  This solar simulator produces a calibrated AM1.5 light that meets the IEC 60904-9:2020 standard using LEDs.  The light meets class AAA in a 15 millimeter diameter area and class ABA in a 25 millimeter diameter area.  An optical breadboard acts as the mechanical base for the device, providing ample space for hard mounting of electronics.  Additionally, this solar simulator provides a serial control interface and shutter capabilities.

\subsection*{Software}

The Python programming language \cite{van1995python} will be used to command the curve tracer and receive I-V points from the measurement circuits.  A simple graphical user interface will be designed that allows user control and real-time visualization of I-V curves.  An MSP430 microcontroller from Texas Instruments will be integrated to control the curve tracer and multiplexer, which will be programmed in C++.  A serial interface command set will be implemented that allows hardware control.  

\section*{Results}

\subsection*{Hardware}

The electronics were designed using KiCad 5.1.10 and were assembled by hand.  The MSP-EXP430FR5969 LaunchPad development kit was integrated for the embedded control, allowing easy adaptation to other LaunchPad development kits.  The main circuits are shown in Figure \ref{fig:circuits} and are flight ready \cite{lutherinreview}.

Figure \ref{fig:a} shows the curve tracer.  The voltage measurement is performed using an OPA2387 operational amplifier, providing an ultra-high precision, zero-drift, low input bias current interface, and characterized from -40$^{\circ}$C to 125$^{\circ}$C.  Two channels provide either a voltage follower or 0.5x amplification.  The current measurement is performed using an INA190A4 current shunt amplifier, providing 200x amplification of the shunt voltage, zero-drift, and characterized from -40$^{\circ}$C to 125$^{\circ}$C.  For the current sensor, a precision reference generator of 1.25V can be chosen using a jumper.  An additional 3V3 precision reference generator is provided for the microcontroller, which is connected in using a jumper.

Figure \ref{fig:b} shows the resistor ladder for varying the load conditions of the PSC.  A cascaded 8-bit binary up/down counter is implemented using a CD4516, with a CD4071 to handle overflow ripple.  The SPST switches control the connection or bypassing of the selected resistor using the ADG841, providing a 370 m${\ohm}$ conduction channel, which is significantly lower than any resistor on the ladder.  The selected resistors are 50${\ohm}$, 250${\ohm}$, 500${\ohm}$, 1k${\ohm}$, 2k${\ohm}$, 4k${\ohm}$, 8k${\ohm}$, and 16k${\ohm}$, from LSB to MSB.  It's important to have each successive resistor to be twice as large as the preceding one, to allow a linear increase as the ladder is incremented.

Figure \ref{fig:c} shows the common-anode multiplexer circuit, which selects the pixel of the PSC to be measured.  A BCD-to-decimal counter is implemented using CD4518 cascaded with a CD4514.  The SPDT switches are implemented using ADG839, with 500 m${\ohm}$ conduction channels, where the normally connected channel (S1) is connected to the discharge path and the normally open channel (S2) is connected to the negative lead of the curve tracer.

\subsection*{Sample Holder}

The sample holder was designed to allow quick switching of PSCs by integrating the sample holder onto two 10 pin headers.  A 3D printed frame is screwed into the circuit board that has rotating fingers that hold the PSC onto the frame.  On the circuit board are gold plated pogo pins that provide electrical contact to the PSC, which the rotating fingers apply pressure to compress the pogo pins with the PSC.  To control the power generating area of the PSC, a light mask is layered on top of the PSC.  The PSC electrical connections are routed out of the circuit board through the 10 pin headers.  The sample holders for the PSC were designed using Fusion 360 and printed on a Sovol SV06 3D printer using white PLA filament.

\subsection*{Software}

To communicate with the Ossila Solar Simulator, a Python library was developed called pyOssila which implements the full command specification for use in scripts \cite{Arce_pyOssila_2023}. Figure \ref{fig:ui} is the user interface developed using tkinter, which is built into Python.  When running a test the following information can be entered: the ID of the PSC, the power generating area of the PSC, and the irradiance being tested.  Additionally, the user can indicate that the curves should be saved using the "Save Curves?" checkbox.  Once those parameters are setup, the "Trace Curve" button can be pressed to initiate the characterization.  As curves are captured from the hardware, the curves are displayed in the plot on the lower half of the display.

For the purposes of comparison, the current density was calculated instead of utilizing only current, yielding a J-V curve, instead of an I-V curve.  The only difference is that the current at each point is divided by the power generating area of the PSC under test.  

The firmware for the MSP430 was written in C++ using the Energia Integrated Development Environment and implements the analog-to-digital conversion, the control of the curve tracer, and control of the multiplexer.  A simple byte-code command structure was implemented, consisting of four commands which are explained in Table \ref{tab:my-table}.

\subsection*{Curve Tracing}

The developed system was used extensively in the Big Red Sat-1 project for characterizing samples provided by the National Renewable Energy Laboratory.  Different discharge loading conditions were tested, which presented an issue for the multiplexer.  The resistive load curve tracer varied from around 2 ${\ohm}$ to 1 M${\ohm}$.  When the discharge resistor was set at any point above 2 ${\ohm}$, there would be leakage from the other pixels, causing a surge in the J-V curve, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:badcurve}.

\begin{figure}[!htb]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=7.5cm, height=7.5cm, keepaspectratio]{badshunt.png}
	\includegraphics[width=7.5cm, height=7.5cm, keepaspectratio]{badshuntreverse.png}
	\caption{The J-V curve would surge from a discharge resistor that was set in the active curve tracing range.  This figure shows the discharge resistor set to 500 ${\ohm}$, and when the curve tracer went below this value, the other pixels would leak into the measurement node and cause a surge in the curve.  The left figure is the forward trace condition and the right figure is the reverse trace condition.}
	\label{fig:badcurve}
\end{figure}

This left only the second best choice for the discharge resistor, which was short circuit.  Once the discharge path has a lower resistance than the short circuit condition of the curve tracer, then no leakage is observed.  So, this resistor was fixed to 0 ${\ohm}$.  After resolving this question, the system was prepared for characterizing samples, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:hardware}.

\begin{figure}[!htb]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=7.5cm, height=7.5cm, keepaspectratio]{X018_LM_plotted_Forward.png}
	\includegraphics[width=7.5cm, height=7.5cm, keepaspectratio]{X018_LM_plotted_Reverse.png}
	\caption{The curves traced on the X018 sample, left is the forward condition and right is the reverse condition.}
	\label{fig:curves}

NREL provided three rounds of PSCs for testing, which were all characterized using this hardware, the data from this testing can be seen here \href{https://github.com/wsarce/Perovskite-Curve-Tracer/tree/main/software/curves}{on GitHub}.  Figure \ref{fig:curves} shows the data for sample X018, which was launched on Big Red Sat-1 \cite{arce2024big} and was the champion PSC that maintained T80 while in low earth orbit, as discussed in \cite{lutherinreview}.  The curves show all six pixels operating with a short circuit current density near 25 $mA$ $cm{^{-2}}$, corresponding to a percent efficiency between 15\% and 18\%, depending on the pixel.  Some hysteresis can be observed between the forward and reverse trace condition, with a slightly more rounded knee on the reverse condition.

\section*{Discussion}

While the curve tracer was successful in completing our mission and performed well, there is always room for improvement.  The multiplexing circuitry was chosen to be compatible with space conditions, where semiconductors are less prone to environmental damage due to low pressure and temperature fluctuations.  It was also deemed less risky to the overall mission.  An alternative would be to follow the approach of \cite{lopez2021design, pulungan2022design} and use electromechanical relays.  

For space environments, this could be risky or require hermetically sealed relays.  The risk comes from the evacuation of the air in relay from the vacuum of space, which can lead to arc-at-break damage \cite{teverovsky1999relay}.  \cite{teverovsky1999relay} demonstrated this failure mode with switching high power antenna feedlines, so it's possible that the lower power of the PSCs would not have the same issues.  For terrestrial systems, or near-space conditions, this would be less likely to be a problem.  This would also make the multiplexing consume more power, but would remove the issue with the leakage that was demonstrated in Figure \ref{fig:badcurve}.  This could improve the lifetime of the PSCs when in storage, as the normally connected node would always be engaged, as opposed to the off-state of the semiconductor SPDT switch.

\section*{Conclusions}

For future missions that investigate PSC performance in space missions, the reported curve tracer provides a low-cost and easily understandable instrument for characterizing PSCs.  The system is fully integrated, with an AM1.5 light source, hardware unit, and software packages for usage, allowing quick turnaround on laboratory usage.  The open-source hardware allows integration into flight computers and allows modifications for new parts as improvement of electronics continues over time.
